📌 Case in One Paragraph
On January 28, 2026, Joseph Rivera was pulled over in San Carlos Park, Lee County, FL for a registration citation. Four Lee County Sheriff's Office deputies responded, physically took Rivera to the ground without resistance, threatened a Baker Act psychiatric hold to coerce compliance, deleted Rivera's phone recording of the entire stop, conducted a warrantless destructive search of his vehicle causing physical damage, and refused to document the use of force. Rivera's girlfriend Hannah recorded the takedown on her phone — those videos are safe. LCSO has since obstructed three separate Chapter 119 records requests using documented, coordinated methods. Eight body cameras are scheduled to auto-purge on April 28, 2026. Rivera has preserved everything, filed with FDLE, DOJ, ACLU, and sent demand letters. He needs an attorney now.

⚖️ Claims — Bullet Summary
🚨 First Amendment — Recording Deleted
  • Rivera recording his own traffic stop — protected First Amendment activity
  • LCSO officer deleted recording directly from Rivera's phone during the stop
  • 11th Circuit controlling: Smith v. City of Cumming — right clearly established, no QI
  • Deletion = destruction of evidence (Fla. Stat. §839.13) + federal spoliation
  • No discipline, no documentation = LCSO ratification of the custom
🚔 Fourth Amendment — Stop Extended + Search
  • Registration citation only — no basis to extend stop beyond issuance
  • Rodriguez v. United States (2015, unanimous SCOTUS) — stop cannot be extended
  • Warrantless destructive vehicle search — physical damage to interior
  • Collins v. Virginia (2018) — warrantless destructive search = Fourth Amendment violation
  • Four officers deployed for a non-criminal registration citation
👊 Excessive Force — Graham v. Connor
  • Ground takedown of non-resistant individual on registration citation
  • All three Graham v. Connor factors favor Rivera: no crime, no resistance, no flight risk
  • Hannah's video shows takedown — no provocation visible
  • Four-officer response = excessive deployment, no documented justification
  • No use-of-force report filed — itself a policy violation and cover-up
🏥 Due Process — Baker Act Coercion
  • Officer threatened involuntary psychiatric hold (Baker Act) to force compliance
  • No statutory basis — Rivera exhibited no criteria for Baker Act
  • Zinermon v. Burch — involuntary commitment requires valid statutory predicate
  • Threat = coercion to waive rights, violates 14th Amendment due process
  • Documented in witness statement under penalty of perjury
🏛️ Monell — Municipal Policy / Custom
  • Three separate documented methods of records obstruction = deliberate policy
  • Body cam purge after written litigation hold = deliberate indifference (Sheriff = final policymaker)
  • No discipline for recording deletion = ratification of custom (City of Canton)
  • Single final-policymaker decision sufficient: Pembaur v. Cincinnati
  • Attorney fee shifting under 42 U.S.C. §1988 — LCSO pays your fees if you prevail
📁 Chapter 119 — Public Records Obstruction
  • Three simultaneous obstruction methods: merged requests, excessive fees, expired portal
  • Fee rate of $144.73/hr exceeds §119.07(4)(d) statutory cap — documented
  • Portal activation expired — LCSO refused to renew, resets clock
  • Separate records request for Feb 8-9 phone incident — also obstructed
  • Mandamus action available in addition to §1983 — dual track
🛡️ QUALIFIED IMMUNITY STATUS
• Recording deletion: QI defeated — Smith v. City of Cumming (11th Cir. 2000) clearly establishes the right in this circuit
• Excessive force on non-resistant subject: QI difficult — Graham factors all favor Rivera, video evidence
• Extended stop: QI defeated — Rodriguez is unanimous SCOTUS (2015), cannot claim ignorance
• Baker Act coercion: QI available for argument but Zinermon statutory predicate requirement is established

💰 42 U.S.C. §1988 — You Get Paid by LCSO if You Win
HOW §1988 WORKS
  • In any successful §1983 civil rights action, the defendant (LCSO) pays your attorney fees
  • Rivera pays nothing out of pocket — contingency case, your fees come from LCSO
  • Fee amount = your reasonable hourly rate × hours worked, paid by the losing government party
  • Courts have approved rates of $300–$600/hr for civil rights attorneys in the 11th Circuit
  • Settlement negotiations often include lump-sum attorney fee component
